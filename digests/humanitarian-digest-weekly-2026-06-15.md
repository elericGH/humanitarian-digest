## 🇦🇺 Australian Priority Alerts

The dominant story requiring an Australian government response this week is the **magnitude 7.8 earthquake that struck Sarangani Province, Mindanao, Philippines on 8 June 2026**, affecting a near neighbour in Australia's immediate region of strategic and humanitarian concern. As of 12 June, 47 people were confirmed dead, 688 injured, 31 missing, and over 346,449 people across 75,324 families were affected. More than 2,964 aftershocks followed, including several above M6.0, complicating access and assessment.

DFAT's humanitarian response posture toward the Indo-Pacific — including the Philippines — has been active in 2026. Australia has previously committed over AUD $14 million in Indo-Pacific disaster response funding spanning Indonesia, Malaysia, the Philippines, Sri Lanka, Thailand and Vietnam. Whether a specific Philippines earthquake response announcement was made in the June 8–15 window could not be confirmed via automated fetch; readers should check the [DFAT News page](https://www.dfat.gov.au/news) and the [Foreign Minister's website](https://www.foreignminister.gov.au) for any statement dated this week.

ACFID's most recent published commentary (29 May 2026) flagged that the 2026–27 Federal Budget maintained the aid envelope but called for stronger investment. ACFID noted mounting pressure from the Middle East conflicts, the Pacific fuel emergency, and regional disasters — all of which intensified this week with the Philippines earthquake. Australian NGO members responding to the Philippines situation should monitor ACFID's [news hub](https://acfid.asn.au/news) for any emergency response coordination updates.

Australia's Pacific partners continue to face compounding economic hardship from the global fuel price crisis. Marshall Islands has declared a 90-day economic emergency, Tuvalu declared a state of emergency amid daily blackouts, and fuel prices in Fiji, Vanuatu and Solomon Islands have risen dramatically, raising costs for disaster response logistics across the region.

## 🌏 Tier 1 — Pacific & Asia-Pacific

### Philippines: 7.8 Magnitude Sarangani Earthquake

The most significant sudden-onset humanitarian event of the week was the **magnitude 7.8 earthquake that struck 32 km offshore of Maasim, Sarangani Province, Mindanao, on 8 June at 7:37am local time**, along the Cotabato Trench at a depth of 33 km. By 12 June, confirmed figures stood at 47 dead, 688 injured, 31 missing, and 346,449 people affected. A total of 2,964 aftershocks were recorded in the first four days, ranging from M1.2 to M6.4, severely hampering search and rescue and access assessment.

Landslides and damaged bridges cut off Glan municipality from ground access, requiring aerial assessment. According to [OCHA's Flash Update #1](https://reliefweb.int/report/philippines/philippines-78m-sarangani-earthquake-flash-update-no-1-8-june-2026), 16,349 people were sheltering in 32 official evacuation centres as of 11 June, with many more in open spaces and makeshift shelters. Save the Children noted that children face particular risks from exposure and the lack of safe, enclosed shelter ([source](https://reliefweb.int/report/philippines/philippines-earthquake-children-displaced-following-strong-quake-focus-must-be-ensuring-safe-shelter)).

UN Women's [Gender Alert #1](https://reliefweb.int/report/philippines/gender-alert-no-1-philippines-78-magnitude-sarangani-earthquake-12-june-2026) highlighted that women and girls face heightened protection risks in displacement settings, particularly with many families forced into open public spaces. International Medical Corps published [Situation Report #1 on 11 June](https://reliefweb.int/report/philippines/philippines-earthquake-situation-report-1-june-11-2026) confirming 75,324 affected families and ongoing rescue operations. OCHA's [Asia-Pacific Weekly Snapshot](https://reliefweb.int/report/philippines/asia-and-pacific-weekly-regional-humanitarian-snapshot-2-8-june-2026) noted the earthquake triggered a Pacific-wide tsunami warning that was later stood down.

The Philippines earthquake is a significant test of the humanitarian system's ability to respond at scale in a middle-income country with its own substantial emergency management capacity. Multiple clusters are being activated and inter-agency coordination is underway.

### Myanmar: Mass Displacement Continues

Myanmar's protracted crisis deepened further this week with ongoing displacement from military airstrikes and ground operations. According to a [multi-agency crisis analysis published 9 June](https://reliefweb.int/report/myanmar/myanmar-crisis-situation-analysis-period-250526-310526), over 44,000 people were displaced in Pakokku and Saw townships in Magway Region since mid-May, and more than 25,000 were displaced in western Bago Region following airstrikes that killed at least 9 civilians in late May.

The food security situation remains dire. Approximately 12 million people face moderate-to-severe food insecurity in 2026. The 2026 Humanitarian Needs and Response Plan (HNRP) appeals for $890 million but remains severely underfunded, with access constraints continuing to prevent aid workers from reaching many affected populations. The pattern of aerial bombardment of civilian areas and deliberate targeting of humanitarian infrastructure is an escalating concern.

### Bhutan: M5.6 Earthquake near Punakha

A magnitude 5.6 earthquake struck near Punakha, Bhutan on 7 June at 23:36 local time, felt widely across the country including Thimphu, with stronger shaking in northern areas. Four aftershocks (M1.6–2.8) followed. No major casualties were reported but structural damage is being assessed. This coincided with the Philippines earthquake, briefly straining regional monitoring capacity. Details are captured in [OCHA's Asia-Pacific Snapshot](https://reliefweb.int/report/philippines/asia-and-pacific-weekly-regional-humanitarian-snapshot-2-8-june-2026).

## 🌍 Tier 2 — Middle East & Africa

### Gaza: INGOs Sound Alarm as Access Laws Tighten

Dozens of humanitarian organisations issued a [joint statement on 10 June](https://reliefweb.int/report/occupied-palestinian-territory/joint-statement-humanitarian-access-gaza-and-registration-law-affecting-ingos) expressing profound concern about catastrophic conditions in Gaza, where aid volumes remain wholly insufficient for a population almost entirely dependent on humanitarian assistance for survival. The statement comes after the Israeli High Court rejected INGOs' appeal against a registration law that severely limits the capacity of international NGOs to operate in Occupied Palestinian Territory. Signatories warned this threatens the legal basis for humanitarian operations and could force further withdrawals from an already depleted response.

UNRWA's [Situation Report #224](https://reliefweb.int/report/occupied-palestinian-territory/unrwa-situation-report-224-humanitarian-crisis-gaza-strip-and-west-bank-including-east-jerusalem-enar) covering days 961–968 of the conflict (to early June 2026) described a plan to rapidly scale up operations and increase lifesaving assistance, though access and security remain fundamental constraints. WHO's [Middle East Escalation Situation Report #9 (11 June)](https://reliefweb.int/report/lebanon/world-health-organization-middle-east-escalation-conflict-global-situation-report-9-11-june-2026) covered renewed hostilities between Israel and Iran on 7–8 June that caused additional casualties and infrastructure damage across the region, noting ongoing communicable disease risks and attacks on healthcare in Lebanon.

### Yemen: Aid Workers Detained, Food Crisis Deepening

As of February 2026, 73 UN staff and dozens of civil society workers remain in Huthi detention, all Yemeni nationals. A [joint statement by Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International and the Cairo Institute published 8 June](https://reliefweb.int/report/yemen/yemen-huthis-should-free-un-civil-society-staff-enar) condemned the ongoing arbitrary detention and called for immediate release, noting one WFP aid worker died in custody in February 2025. HRW said the detentions are directly curtailing lifesaving assistance to populations in critical need.

The food security crisis is worsening. An [IPC analysis published 3 June](https://reliefweb.int/report/yemen/nearly-half-population-government-controlled-areas-yemen-face-acute-food-insecurity-humanitarian-support-sharply-declines) found 47% of the population in government-controlled areas — approximately 5 million people — face IPC Phase 3+ acute food insecurity, including 1.4 million in Emergency (Phase 4). During the June–September lean season this is projected to rise to 5.4 million (51%). A sharp decline in humanitarian funding is a key driver, creating a dangerous gap between needs and response.

### Somalia: Health Facilities Closing, Famine Risk Rising

Nearly 500 primary healthcare facilities across Somalia have closed due to severe funding shortfalls, according to [CARE published 11 June](https://reliefweb.int/report/somalia/somalia-health-centers-close-due-aid-cuts-famine-looms-care). Almost 2 million children are acutely malnourished, including 500,000 with severe acute malnutrition. In Galmudug region alone, 11 health centres have shut, ending mobile clinics, cash support and water programs. The cost of therapeutic milk — a lifesaving treatment for severely malnourished children — has risen 44% in two years. CARE warned that without urgent intervention, Somalia is sliding toward famine conditions.

The closures reflect a global pattern of humanitarian funding shortfalls, driven in part by reduced US Government engagement in multilateral humanitarian financing and increased competition for donor dollars across simultaneous crises.

### South Sudan: Children Surviving on Leaves

Families in eastern Jonglei state are surviving on leaves and water lilies after three months of escalating violence triggered a suspension of aid operations and mass displacement, [Save the Children reported on 9 June](https://reliefweb.int/report/south-sudan/children-surviving-leaves-and-water-lilies-conflict-drives-parts-south-sudan-brink-famine-save-children). Save the Children suspended operations in Akobo East; a February 2026 attack on their Walgak station also shut down services. FEWS NET projects 50–55% of South Sudan's population will need humanitarian food assistance by December 2026 — one of the highest proportions of any country globally.

### DRC: Ebola Response Under Strain

The Ebola outbreak in Ituri Province, DRC, faces critical supply shortages as temporary border closures with Uganda and Rwanda, combined with insecurity along transport routes, cut off deliveries of protective equipment for health workers. [IRC reported on 9 June](https://reliefweb.int/report/democratic-republic-congo/ebola-response-faces-critical-supply-shortages-border-closures-hamper-essential-aid) that at least 16 health workers have contracted Ebola and protective equipment stocks were expected to run out within days at the time of publication. A separate IRC warning flagged that with only 20% of contacts currently being traced, the true scale of the outbreak is likely significantly larger than official figures suggest.

### Mali: Emergency Food Insecurity in Kidal

Food insecurity in Kidal, Mali, has deteriorated to IPC Phase 4 (Emergency), according to [FEWS NET published 11 June](https://reliefweb.int/report/mali/mali-key-message-update-food-insecurity-deteriorates-emergency-ipc-phase-4-kidal-amid-severe-conflict-may-september-2026). Coordinated attacks by the Azawad Liberation Front (FLA) and JNIM on 25 April sharply reduced trade flows from Algeria, while military bombardments disrupted movement. Staple cereal prices rose 15–42% above five-year averages; fuel prices rose 42–60% in affected areas.

### Sudan: Bracing for Worsening Malnutrition

About 19 million people — two in five Sudanese — face acute food insecurity, with the ongoing conflict having caused the world's largest displacement crisis at approximately 14 million people. In Gedaref state (once Sudan's breadbasket), overcrowded nutrition clinics are treating growing numbers of severely malnourished children as the pre-harvest lean season intensifies. [Save the Children's report from 1 June](https://reliefweb.int/report/sudan/sudan-braces-worsening-child-malnutrition-pre-harvest-season-starts) warns conditions will deteriorate further in the weeks ahead. The Sudan Humanitarian Fund allocated USD 40 million in its first 2026 standard allocation, though this covers a fraction of identified needs.

## 🌐 Tier 3 — Rest of the World

### Global: First Decline in Forced Displacement in a Decade

UNHCR's flagship [Global Trends: Forced Displacement 2025 report, published 11 June](https://reliefweb.int/report/world/global-trends-forced-displacement-2025-enar), reveals a 3% decline in global refugee numbers to 41.6 million — the first such decrease in over a decade. However, UNHCR's chief cautioned the number masks deep structural problems: 70% of refugees remain in long-term exile with no durable solutions, and 14.7 million people returned in 2025, many under pressure rather than out of genuine choice. UNHCR launched a new initiative aimed at halving long-term displacement over the next decade.

### Global: 115–125 Million People Projected to Need Food Aid by December 2026

FEWS NET's [Food Assistance Outlook Brief for June 2026](https://reliefweb.int/report/world/food-assistance-outlook-brief-june-2026) projects 115–125 million people across FEWS NET presence countries will require humanitarian food assistance by December 2026. Sudan, DRC, Nigeria and Yemen each account for more than 10% of total needs. South Sudan faces the highest population share (50–55%), followed by Sudan (40–45%) and Yemen (35–40%). Funding gaps at the scale required to prevent famine remain critical across all four countries.

### Haiti: Fresh Displacement in Port-au-Prince

Armed clashes across Port-au-Prince neighbourhoods (Village de Dieu, Cité de l'Éternel, Martissant–Bréard–Saint-Bernadette) since 1 June displaced an estimated 5,054 individuals (1,585 households), according to the [OCHA Latin America and Caribbean Weekly Update of 12 June](https://reliefweb.int/report/mexico/latin-america-caribbean-weekly-situation-update-12-june-2026). Haiti continues to face compounding crises of gang violence, political instability and weak service provision. The Multinational Security Support Mission (MSS) led by Kenya has had limited impact on gang control of key infrastructure.

### Syria: Euphrates Flooding Displaces Thousands

Flooding of the Euphrates River displaced approximately 700 families (around 2,000 children) in Deir-ez-Zor and Raqqa governorates of Syria, according to [UNICEF's situation report of 10 June](https://reliefweb.int/report/syrian-arab-republic/unicef-syria-humanitarian-situation-report-euphrates-flooding-08-june-2026). Forty-nine water treatment plants were knocked out of service across the two governorates. [WFP's satellite-based flood extent assessment](https://reliefweb.int/report/syrian-arab-republic/wfp-satellite-based-flood-extent-agricultural-impact-assessment-euphrates-river-flood-deir-ez-zor-governorate-june-2026) documented the agricultural impacts, with crops affected at a critical point in the growing season.

### Rohingya: 2026 Joint Response Plan Seeks USD 710.5 Million

UN agencies and partners have launched the 2026 Joint Response Plan for Rohingya refugees in Bangladesh, seeking USD 710.5 million — 26% lower than 2025, reflecting what agencies describe as a minimum-lifesaving envelope only. Of 1.2 million Rohingya refugees residing in Bangladesh's Cox's Bazar camps, approximately 150,000 arrived since early 2024. The plan allocates USD 247.3 million for food and USD 128 million for shelter. Details are outlined in the [multi-agency funding appeal](https://reliefweb.int/report/bangladesh/un-and-partners-appeal-usd-7105-million-meet-critical-needs-rohingya-refugees-and-bangladeshi-host-communities-enbn).

### Mexico/Central America: Tropical Storms Boris and Cristina

Tropical Storm Boris formed on 8 June off Guerrero, Mexico, making landfall on 9 June and causing one death, two people missing, and flash flooding across Guerrero, Oaxaca, Colima and Chiapas. Tropical Storm Cristina formed the same day approximately 160 km southwest of Managua, Nicaragua, posing additional risk to Central American communities already strained by recurring climate shocks. Both storms are documented in the [OCHA Latin America update of 12 June](https://reliefweb.int/report/mexico/latin-america-caribbean-weekly-situation-update-12-june-2026).

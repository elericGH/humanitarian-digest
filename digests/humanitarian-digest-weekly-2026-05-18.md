# 🌍 Weekly Humanitarian Digest — 11 May 2026 – 18 May 2026
*Produced Monday, 18 May 2026 | Australian Interest Priority Edition*

---

## 🇦🇺 Australian Priority Alerts

The week brought continued calls for Australia to step into a leadership role as global official development assistance contracts sharply. The Australian Council for International Development (ACFID) has been publicly urging the incoming government to treat the global aid retreat — particularly the near-total withdrawal of US funding through USAID — as a strategic opening rather than a cue to follow. With traditional donors pulling back and multilateral funding gaps widening across Gaza, Sudan and the Pacific, ACFID argues the moment calls for Australia to expand its ODA commitments, particularly toward the Indo-Pacific neighbourhood. The peak body's "Australia's opportunity to lead as global aid plummets" messaging has framed the case in both humanitarian and strategic-interest terms. ([ACFID News](https://acfid.asn.au/news-and-media/))

Australia's obligations under International Humanitarian Law also remained in focus, with ACFID maintaining its standing call for the Australian Government to press for protection of civilians and adherence to IHL amid ongoing escalation in the Middle East. The organisation's consolidated appeal for Middle East humanitarian response specifically addresses the need for sustained funding to Gaza, Lebanon and the occupied West Bank. ([ACFID Consolidated Appeal – Middle East Humanitarian Crisis](https://acfid.asn.au/consolidated-appeal-middle-east-humanitarian-crisis/))

Palestinian aid access remained a specific Australian advocacy pressure point. ACFID has flagged urgent action needed to prevent an aid shutdown across Palestine, noting that blockages at Israeli-controlled crossings are severely constraining delivery. This connects directly to the OCHA reporting this week that only one in every two aid trucks from Egypt was able to offload at Gaza perimeter crossings in the first eleven days of May — a figure that carries direct implications for Australian-funded NGO partners operating inside the Strip. ([ACFID – Urgent Action Needed to Prevent Aid Shutdown Across Palestine](https://acfid.asn.au/urgent-action-needed-to-prevent-aid-shutdown-across-palestine/))

The Emergency Action Alliance Australia (emergencyaction.org.au) was unavailable for access during this monitoring cycle. DFAT's humanitarian announcements portal was not accessible. These are noted as sources unreachable this run; monitoring will resume next cycle.

## 🌏 Tier 1 — Pacific & Asia-Pacific

**Papua New Guinea — Cyclone Maila response deepens**

The humanitarian response to Severe Tropical Cyclone Maila continued to expand this week, with situation report number seven (dated 8 May 2026) confirming that assessments have now reached 230 wards across 28 local-level government areas, 10 districts and 5 provinces. Preliminary findings put the total number of people affected at over 119,000, with more than 107,000 in need of humanitarian assistance and 49 confirmed fatalities. Priority needs include food assistance, shelter and non-food items, WASH services, health support and logistics to reach isolated communities. The National Disaster Centre (NDC) is leading coordination through the National Emergency Operations Centre and is transitioning toward sub-national led recovery, though logistics constraints persist in hard-to-reach areas. Given that the storm struck just before the monitoring window, its response trajectory remains a Tier 1 watch for the weeks ahead. ([ReliefWeb — PNG Cyclone Maila Situation Report No. 07](https://reliefweb.int/report/papua-new-guinea/papua-new-guinea-severe-tropical-cyclone-maila-disaster-management-team-situation-report-no-07-08-may-2026))

**Pacific — Fuel crisis threatening childhood outcomes; Biketawa Declaration under consideration**

A striking UNICEF analysis published 7 May documents how Strait of Hormuz-related fuel price increases are already translating into concrete deprivations for Pacific families: reduced meal frequency, children missing school, deferred medical care and shrinking household purchasing power. The Pacific's structural vulnerability — near-total import dependence for food, fuel, medicines and basic supplies — means global price shocks are amplified at the household level faster than almost anywhere else. Most significantly, the report notes that Pacific leaders are considering invoking the Biketawa Declaration, the region's highest collective crisis framework last activated for COVID-19, suggesting that national governments themselves view the situation as crossing a threshold for collective regional action. ([ReliefWeb — When the price of fuel becomes the price of childhood: Pacific families in a crisis](https://reliefweb.int/report/world/when-price-fuel-becomes-price-childhood-pacific-families-crisis))

Seismic activity in the Pacific also warranted monitoring this week. A magnitude 5.7 earthquake struck Solomon Islands on 16 May, and a magnitude 5.7 earthquake was recorded near Tonga on 12 May. Neither triggered a Pacific-wide tsunami alert, but both underscore the region's persistent exposure to sudden-onset geological hazards. Volcanic activity at Ambae, Vanuatu — active since February 2026 — continues. ([GDACS](https://gdacs.org))

**Myanmar — civil conflict and Middle East spillover converging**

Myanmar's crisis continued on multiple fronts. A situation analysis covering 27 April to 3 May (published 12 May 2026) underscored that the country's civil war, now entering its sixth year since the February 2021 coup, continues to displace and impoverish millions, with the Tatmadaw's conduct generating ongoing protection concerns. A complementary report published 8 May specifically examines how the Hormuz crisis is compounding Myanmar's food security vulnerability: with the country importing over 90% of its fuel needs, rising oil prices are driving up transportation and logistics costs across the supply chain, in turn pushing food prices higher and eroding household purchasing power for the country's most marginalised communities. Fertiliser price surges linked to the same regional instability are also threatening agricultural inputs ahead of the planting season. The convergence of conflict, economic fragility and external price shocks creates a compounding humanitarian risk that warrants sustained attention. ([ReliefWeb — Myanmar Crisis Situation Analysis, 27 Apr–3 May 2026](https://reliefweb.int/report/myanmar/myanmar-crisis-situation-analysis-period-270426-030526)) | ([ReliefWeb — Spillover Effects: How the Middle East crisis threatens food security, focus Myanmar](https://reliefweb.int/report/myanmar/spillover-effects-how-middle-east-crisis-threatens-food-security-focus-myanmar))

**Afghanistan & Pakistan-Afghanistan returns**

An updated Red Cross/IFRC operation report (published 14 May) on the August 2025 Jalalabad earthquake — which registered 6.0 magnitude and triggered significant aftershocks of 5.2, 4.5 and 5.6 — confirmed that response operations remain active, with recovery and reconstruction needs extending into 2026 across Kunar, Nangarhar, Laghman and Nuristan provinces. Separately, the Pakistan-Afghanistan returns emergency response situation report number 51 (as of 9 May) tracked ongoing large-scale movements of Afghan refugees being returned from Pakistan. Between January and April 2026, UNHCR recorded substantial voluntary repatriation flows, though the phrase "voluntary" is contested given Pakistan's policy pressure on the Afghan refugee population. This movement creates compounding humanitarian demands at border crossing points and in receiving communities inside Afghanistan. ([ReliefWeb — Afghanistan Earthquake Operation Update #4](https://reliefweb.int/report/afghanistan/afghanistan-earthquake-operation-update-4-mdraf019)) | ([ReliefWeb — Pakistan-Afghanistan Returns Emergency Response #51](https://reliefweb.int/report/afghanistan/pakistan-afghanistan-returns-emergency-response-51-9-may-2026))

## 🌍 Tier 2 — Middle East & Africa

**Lebanon — ceasefire fragile, Flash Appeal critically underfunded**

Despite a ceasefire announced on 17 April, OCHA's Lebanon Flash Update #26 (covering the period to 14 May, published 16 May) paints a picture of deep instability. Hostilities continued across southern Lebanon throughout the reporting period, with renewed displacement orders announced for at least 18 additional localities. Ten new emergency shelters were opened within a three-day period in response to growing displacement. The cumulative toll since the escalation began on 2 March now stands at 2,896 killed and 8,824 injured. An attack on a healthcare facility during this period killed two healthcare workers and injured another, underscoring the persistent threat to health infrastructure. Critically, the Flash Appeal is only 42 per cent funded, placing essential services — including shelter, protection and primary healthcare — at immediate risk of interruption. The UNHCR Protection Snapshot (16 April to 8 May, published 15 May) documents displacement patterns post-escalation, with protection concerns elevated particularly for women, children and people with disabilities in newly displaced communities. ([ReliefWeb — Lebanon Flash Update #26](https://reliefweb.int/report/lebanon/lebanon-flash-update-26-escalation-hostilities-lebanon-14-may-2026)) | ([ReliefWeb — Lebanon Protection Snapshot](https://reliefweb.int/report/lebanon/lebanon-protection-snapshot-displacement-post-escalation-16-apr-08-may-2026))

**Gaza / Occupied Palestinian Territory — aid access worsening, injury toll mounts**

The OCHA Humanitarian Situation Report for the occupied Palestinian territory (published 15 May) documents conditions remaining dire, with most of Gaza's population displaced and exposure to health and environmental risks persistent. WHO estimates that over 43,000 people have sustained life-changing injuries, with rehabilitation services severely overstretched. On aid access: only one in every two trucks entering from Egypt was able to offload at Israeli-controlled crossings in the first eleven days of May, based on Logistics Cluster tracking data — a dramatic constraint on the ability of partners to replenish depleted supplies. In the West Bank, nearly 90 per cent of the 45 Palestinian-owned structures demolished between 5 and 11 May were used for residential purposes, reflecting a continuing pattern of forced displacement. Bread production restoration and early market recovery programs are providing some relief, but remain fragile. ([ReliefWeb — OPT Humanitarian Situation Report 15 May 2026](https://reliefweb.int/report/occupied-palestinian-territory/opt-humanitarian-situation-report-15-may-2026))

**Sudan — 19.5 million in acute food insecurity, famine risk persists**

A joint FAO, WFP and UNICEF warning issued 15 May confirmed that nearly 19.5 million people — approximately two in every five Sudanese — are currently facing crisis levels of acute food insecurity (IPC Phase 3 or above). Conflict, displacement and restricted humanitarian access are the primary drivers. While the latest IPC analysis did not classify any area as currently in Famine (IPC Phase 5), conditions were described as "extremely concerning," with famine risk persisting in the most affected areas. Over 825,000 children across Sudan face risk of death from severe acute malnutrition in 2026 alone. Humanitarian access blockages and ongoing conflict in multiple states continue to constrain the ability of partners to reach people in need. ([ReliefWeb — Risk of Famine Persists as Nearly 19.5 Million People Face Acute Food Insecurity in Sudan](https://reliefweb.int/report/sudan/risk-famine-persists-nearly-195-million-people-face-acute-food-insecurity-sudan-0))

**South Sudan — famine risk counties, returnees, aid access attacks**

OCHA's South Sudan Humanitarian Update covering 1–15 May (published 17 May) highlighted three converging pressures. First, tens of thousands of people are returning to Akobo in Jonglei State from displacement sites in Ethiopia, triggering a scaled-up humanitarian response in an area with a fragile calm. Second, a credible risk of famine persists in four counties across Upper Nile and Jonglei States — including Akobo — under a worst-case scenario of escalating conflict and further displacement. Third, and of particular operational concern, humanitarian access incidents are intensifying: confiscation of supplies, detention of aid workers and movement restrictions have all been reported across Upper Nile, Jonglei and Central Equatoria states, representing a significant constraint on the ability to respond to needs. ([ReliefWeb — South Sudan Humanitarian Update 1–15 May 2026](https://reliefweb.int/report/south-sudan/south-sudan-humanitarian-update-1-15-may-2026))

**Somalia — IRC warning of catastrophe as IPC signals renewed famine**

The International Rescue Committee issued a stark warning on 15 May that Somalia is once again approaching catastrophic levels of hunger, based on new IPC projections. The compounding factors are stark: drought, commodity price inflation driven in part by the Hormuz crisis, and supply chain disruptions. Sugar prices have increased 13.3%, rice 9.6%, flour 16%, cooking oil 21.4% and milk 42% relative to baseline. Fertiliser prices have surged dramatically due to regional instability, threatening agricultural production at a critical moment for farming communities. The convergence of reduced harvests, inflated import costs and sustained displacement is recreating the conditions that preceded the 2011 and 2017 famine declarations. ([ReliefWeb — IRC Warns Somalia Is on the Brink of Catastrophe](https://reliefweb.int/report/somalia/irc-warns-somalia-brink-catastrophe-new-ipc-projections-signal-renewed-famine-risk-irc))

**Syria — progress real but fragile; funding falling faster than needs**

OCHA Under-Secretary-General Tom Fletcher briefed the UN Security Council on Syria on 15 May, describing the situation as "critical yet promising." Violence has decreased, sanctions have eased, humanitarian access has improved and returns of refugees and internally displaced people are increasing. However, funding is falling faster than needs, and Fletcher warned that delayed recovery will ultimately cost more lives and more money. Approximately two-thirds of Syria's population — over 15 million people, the majority women, girls and children — require humanitarian assistance in 2026. Current funding levels mean only a fraction of those needs will be met. ([ReliefWeb — Tom Fletcher Security Council Briefing on Syria, 15 May 2026](https://reliefweb.int/report/syrian-arab-republic/mr-tom-fletcher-under-secretary-general-humanitarian-affairs-and-emergency-relief-coordinator-briefing-security-council-humanitarian-situation-syria-15-may-2026))

**West Africa / Sahel — pastoral stress, climate finance gaps**

The Sahel Bi-monthly Pastoral Surveillance Bulletin (February–March period, published 15 May) documented the transition from cool dry to hot dry season, with temporary surface water points drying up and significant forage resources destroyed by bushfires. Pastoral resource availability ranges from sufficient to slightly insufficient across the region, with atypical southward livestock movements creating further management complexity. A concurrent report advocated for strengthened climate finance for the region in response to intensifying mobility pressures, reflecting the structural link between pastoral stress, conflict over resources, and displacement in the Sahel corridor. ([ReliefWeb — Sahel Pastoral Surveillance Bulletin No. 37](https://reliefweb.int/report/world/bi-monthly-pastoral-surveillance-bulletin-sahel-ndeg37-february-march-2026))

## 🌐 Tier 3 — Rest of the World

**Haiti — Artibonite escalation, record displacement**

OCHA's Haiti Flash Update (published 15 May) documented the ongoing deterioration in the Artibonite department following armed attacks beginning 2 May 2026. As of 15 May, 4,419 people had been displaced from the May 2 attacks, with additional population movements continuing after attacks on 8–9 May in the commune of Saint-Marc killed at least six people and left nine with gunshot injuries. Affected localities included Grand Bérard, Beau Fort, Lacouture, Pitralle and surrounding areas. The Artibonite escalation is unfolding against a backdrop of record hemispheric displacement: a Latin America and Caribbean weekly situation update (15 May) noted that the Americas recorded nearly 5.9 million internal displacements in 2025 — the second-highest figure in a decade — with Haiti alone accounting for approximately 977,000, representing over 80 per cent of global displacement attributed to criminal violence. ([ReliefWeb — Haiti Violence and Displacement Artibonite Flash Update No. 2](https://reliefweb.int/report/haiti/haiti-violence-and-displacement-artibonite-flash-update-no-2-15-may-2026)) | ([ReliefWeb — Latin America and Caribbean Weekly Situation Update 15 May 2026](https://reliefweb.int/report/haiti/latin-america-caribbean-weekly-situation-update-15-may-2026))

**Ukraine — Q1 2026 protection update**

UNHCR's Ukraine Protection Update No. 10 (Q1 2026, published 15 May) documented ongoing tailored protection support for internally displaced persons and returnees, with an emphasis on strengthening inclusive national systems and encouraging community participation in decision-making. The update reflects UNHCR's continued integration of protection responses with national service capacity-building, with Memoranda of Understanding supporting systematic handover toward national systems over the medium term. ([ReliefWeb — UNHCR Ukraine Protection Update No. 10, Q1 2026](https://reliefweb.int/report/ukraine/unhcr-ukraine-protection-update-10-q1-2026))

**El Niño watch**

The Latin America and Caribbean weekly situation update noted an 82 per cent probability of El Niño conditions emerging by May–July 2026, with implications for food security, water availability and disaster risk across multiple regions including parts of the Pacific, East Africa and South Asia. Monitoring agencies are tracking potential impacts on agricultural seasons that would compound existing food insecurity in already fragile contexts. ([ReliefWeb — Latin America and Caribbean Weekly Situation Update 15 May 2026](https://reliefweb.int/report/haiti/latin-america-caribbean-weekly-situation-update-15-may-2026))

## 🚨 Sudden Onset Hazards

This was a highly active week for sudden-onset hazards across multiple regions.

**Earthquakes:** A magnitude 6.7 earthquake struck Japan on 15 May. Antigua and Barbuda experienced a magnitude 6.0 quake on 16 May. Indonesia recorded a magnitude 6.2 earthquake on 14 May. Two Pacific events — a magnitude 5.7 in Solomon Islands (16 May) and a magnitude 5.7 near Tonga (12 May) — underscore persistent seismic exposure in the region. None of these events triggered major international tsunami warnings, though national-level responses and assessments are ongoing. ([GDACS](https://gdacs.org))

**Floods:** Indonesia recorded flooding on 17 May. Yemen, already in a protracted humanitarian crisis, recorded floods on 15 May, adding to existing WASH and shelter pressures. Türkiye (14 May), Peru and Pakistan (both 11 May), Colombia (10 May) and the United States (8 May) also recorded flood events during the monitoring period. Pakistan's flooding is occurring in a context of ongoing humanitarian need and raises concern about compounding impacts on already vulnerable displaced communities. ([GDACS](https://gdacs.org))

**Volcanoes:** Mount Ibu in Indonesia remained active as of 10 May. Dukono, also in Indonesia, recorded activity on 8 May. Mayon in the Philippines, which has been an ongoing concern, showed activity as of 3 May. Ambae volcano in Vanuatu has been active since February 2026. ([GDACS](https://gdacs.org))
